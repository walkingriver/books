The #1 Communication Mistake That Almost Cost Me My Career

And how you can avoid making it

The email that nearly derailed my career wasn't rude. It wasn't unprofessional. It wasn't even wrong.

It was sent in anger.

Here's what I learned—and why the email you send when you're upset is the one you'll regret forever.

The Incident

I was working on a critical project with a tight deadline. Another team made a change that broke our integration. No warning. No heads-up. Just a broken build on a Friday afternoon.

I was furious.

I fired off an email explaining exactly what they'd done wrong, exactly how it had affected us, and exactly how unprofessional I thought it was to push a breaking change without communication.

Everything I wrote was factually accurate.

It was still a mistake.

The Fallout

By Monday morning, my email had been forwarded to multiple directors. What started as a technical issue became a political one. Instead of discussing the problem, we were discussing my "tone" and "approach."

The other team got defensive. My team had to spend time defending me. The actual problem—the broken integration—became a footnote.

I'd been right. And it didn't matter.

The Lesson

Here's what I wish someone had told me:

The email you send when you're angry is never the right email to send.

It's not that anger is wrong. Anger is often appropriate. Something genuinely frustrating happened, and you're right to be frustrated.

But when you write in anger, several things happen:

  1. You focus on blame, not solutions. The email becomes about assigning fault rather than fixing the problem.
  2. Your tone bleeds through. Even if you're careful with your words, the reader can feel the emotion behind them.
  3. You create defensiveness. People who feel attacked don't collaborate. They defend.
  4. You leave a paper trail. Unlike a conversation, an email can be forwarded, screenshotted, and remembered forever.

The Rule I Now Follow

When I'm upset about something, I follow a simple rule:

Write the email. Don't send it. Wait 24 hours.

Here's how it works:

Step 1: Write It

Open a draft. Write everything you want to say. Don't hold back. Get it all out. This is therapeutic—and it clarifies your thinking.

Step 2: Save It

Close the draft. Do not put anyone in the "To" field. (Better yet, write it in a notes app where you can't accidentally send it.)

Step 3: Wait

Go do something else. Sleep on it. The distance changes everything.

Step 4: Revise

When you come back, you'll almost always want to change something. Often, you'll realize the email isn't necessary at all—or that a conversation would work better.

What to Write Instead

When you do need to address a frustrating situation in writing, focus on:

  • The impact, not the blame. "This change broke our build" instead of "You broke our build."
  • The future, not the past. "How can we prevent this next time?" instead of "Why didn't you warn us?"
  • Curiosity, not assumptions. "Can you help me understand the timeline?" instead of "You obviously didn't think this through."

You can be direct without being aggressive. You can be clear without being cruel.

When to Pick Up the Phone

Some situations shouldn't be handled over email at all.

If you're genuinely angry, and the issue is significant, consider a phone call or face-to-face conversation instead. Here's why:

  • You can hear tone and adjust in real-time
  • The other person can ask clarifying questions
  • Misunderstandings get resolved faster
  • There's no permanent record of your worst moment

Email is a terrible medium for conflict. It's great for information; it's terrible for emotion.

The Bottom Line

You will get angry at work. Things will go wrong. People will make mistakes that affect you.

How you respond in those moments defines your reputation more than any technical achievement ever will.

The email you send in anger is the one you'll regret forever. The email you send 24 hours later is the one that might actually solve the problem.

Ask me how I know.
