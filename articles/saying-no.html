What 30 Years in Tech Taught Me About Saying No

The skill no one teaches but everyone needs

Early in my career, I said yes to everything.

Extra project? Yes. Weekend work? Yes. Task that wasn't my job? Yes. Meeting that could have been an email? Yes.

I thought this was how you got ahead. Show willingness. Demonstrate commitment. Be the person who never says no.

I was wrong.

Here's what I've learned about saying no—and more importantly, how to do it without destroying your reputation.

The Problem with Always Saying Yes

When you never say no, several things happen:

  1. You become the path of least resistance. People bring you work not because you're the right person, but because you won't push back.
  2. Your important work suffers. Every yes is a no to something else. Usually something that matters more.
  3. You train people to expect unlimited capacity. Then when you finally do say no, it feels like a betrayal.
  4. You burn out. Eventually, something breaks. Usually you.

I've watched talented people derail their careers not because they lacked skill, but because they couldn't set boundaries. They became reliable for saying yes—and unreliable for everything else.

The Magic Phrase

The phrase that changed how I say no:

"I can do that. Here's what I'd need to drop to make room for it."

This one sentence transformed my relationship with my managers. Here's why it works:

It Shows Willingness

You're not refusing. You're saying you can do it. This immediately defuses the tension that comes with "no."

It Acknowledges Reality

There's a cost to every yes. By naming it explicitly, you're being honest about your capacity without being defensive about it.

It Shifts the Decision

Instead of you being the bad guy who said no, your manager has to make a choice. They have to decide what's actually most important.

Often, when forced to choose, they discover the new request isn't as urgent as it seemed. Or they find someone else. Or they adjust the timeline.

Variations That Work

Depending on the situation, here are other ways to frame the same idea:

  • "Happy to help—which of these should I deprioritize?"
  • "I can do X by Friday or Y by Wednesday. Which matters more?"
  • "If this is the priority, I'll need to loop in [name] on [other project]."
  • "I'm committed to [current project] until [date]. Could this wait until then?"

All of these accomplish the same thing: they make the invisible costs visible.

The Line You Won't Cross

There's another kind of no that's even more important: the no that protects your integrity.

Early in my career, I was asked to do something I felt was wrong. Not illegal—just ethically questionable. The kind of thing that happens in corporate environments where the pressure to deliver overrides good judgment.

I said no.

It was uncomfortable. It created tension. For a while, I wondered if it would affect my career.

But here's what I learned: Your integrity is worth more than any job.

The people who remember you for standing your ground are the ones you want to work with again. The people who hold it against you are the ones you should avoid.

How to Say No to Your Boss

Saying no to peers is one thing. Saying no to your manager is scarier—but the same principles apply.

1. Don't React Immediately

When asked to take on something new, your instinct might be to either say yes reflexively or push back defensively. Neither is ideal.

Instead: "Let me check my current commitments and get back to you by end of day."

This buys you time to think clearly.

2. Come with Data

"I'm currently committed to X, Y, and Z, with deadlines on [dates]. Here's how adding this would affect those."

Data removes emotion from the conversation.

3. Offer Alternatives

"I can't take this on this week, but I could start next Monday. Alternatively, [colleague] might have capacity."

You're not just saying no—you're helping solve the problem.

4. Be Direct When Necessary

Sometimes you just have to say it clearly:

"I'm not going to be able to do this well if I take on anything else right now. I'd rather be honest about that than overpromise and underdeliver."

Most reasonable managers respect this.

When No Isn't an Option

Let's be realistic: sometimes you don't have a choice. The deadline is real. The client is important. The crisis is genuine.

In those cases, say yes—but set expectations:

"I can get this done by Friday, but I'll need to deprioritize [X] and I may need to work some extra hours. I want to flag that this pace isn't sustainable long-term."

You're committing to the short-term need while planting a seed about boundaries.

The Meta-Lesson

The real lesson isn't about saying no. It's about making invisible costs visible.

Most managers don't know your full workload. They're not trying to overwork you—they just don't have visibility into what's on your plate.

When you make tradeoffs explicit, you're not being difficult. You're helping them make better decisions.

And that's what good professionals do.
