Why "The Answer is Yes" Changed How I Approach Work

The mindset shift that opens doors you didn't know existed

Early in your career, you'll face opportunities that terrify you. Some will be exciting. Some will be boring. Some will leave you with no idea how you could possibly accept and succeed.

I want to share a mindset shift that changed everything for me: The answer is yes.

Not always. Not blindly. But far more often than your instincts suggest.

The Relationship Lesson

I first encountered this idea in relationship advice. The suggestion was simple: whenever your spouse asks if you want to do something, the default answer should be yes.

At first, this seems absurd. The answer can't always be yes. But consider:

  • Do you want to try that new restaurant?
  • Should we go to that movie I've been wanting to see?
  • Would you like to visit my parents this weekend?

You might not be enthusiastic about all of these. That's fine. Your initial lack of interest doesn't have to be a barrier.

When you say yes, you're investing in your relationship. You might discover you enjoy something you never would have tried on your own. Even if you don't love the activity, you'll appreciate the time together and the joy it brings your partner.

The Professional Application

The same principle applies at work. Consider these requests from your manager:

  • Would you be interested in presenting at the team meeting?
  • Do you want to take on this challenging project?
  • Would you like to mentor a new employee?
  • Do you want to learn a new technology for this initiative?

Your initial hesitation doesn't have to stop you. When you say yes to professional opportunities, you're investing in your career and your relationships with colleagues.

You might discover a hidden talent. You might develop a skill you never knew you needed. Even if the activity isn't your favorite, you'll gain experience, build connections, and increase your visibility.

You never know which opportunity might lead to a promotion, a new connection, or a career-defining moment.

When Saying Yes Changed Everything

Many years ago, I was working for Compaq (remember them?) in New Hampshire. Our team was talented but small—five people with overlapping skills.

One day, our manager asked us a question that demoralized everyone: Could we replace an aging content management system called ActiveAnswers? It was a mess of Perl scripts and text files. The original developers were gone. The system was huge and difficult to maintain.

Could we do it with just our small team?

We spent a weekend researching options. Over lunch on Monday, we decided we couldn't pull it off. We would tell our boss no.

On the way to the meeting, I stopped everyone in the hallway.

"Let's tell him yes," I said.

My teammates were incredulous. "We just decided we can't do it."

My argument was simple:

If we try, we might fail. In that case, we're no worse off than we are now. But if we don't try, failure is guaranteed. What if we try and succeed?

We debated back and forth. Eventually, I won them over. We walked into the meeting exuding confidence we probably didn't feel.

What happened? Over the next few years, we succeeded. We replaced ActiveAnswers with a dynamically generated website with a WYSIWYG editor. After the HP-Compaq merger, our version was selected as a core part of hp.com.

That decision launched careers.

Best Case, Worst Case

Let's be honest: things won't always work out that well.

Worst case scenarios:

  • You might fail publicly
  • You might waste time on something that doesn't pan out
  • You might disappoint your manager
  • You might work long hours on something that goes nowhere

Best case scenarios:

  • You might surprise yourself and succeed beyond expectations
  • You might discover hidden talents or develop new skills
  • You might build stronger relationships with colleagues
  • You might gain visibility and open doors to new opportunities
  • Even if you don't fully succeed, you'll learn valuable lessons

Here's the key insight: In most worst-case scenarios, you're not significantly worse off than before.

Yes, you might have wasted some time—but you gained experience. Yes, you might have failed—but you learned something. Yes, you might have disappointed someone—but you showed initiative.

The best case scenarios, on the other hand, can be transformative.

The Limits

Of course, there are limits.

If something is genuinely dangerous, harmful, or goes against your core values, the answer is no. If you're truly exhausted or dealing with a crisis, it's reasonable to ask if it can wait.

I think of a principle from my days as a scout leader: If the boys wanted to do something, as long as it was legal, moral, ethical, safe, and affordable, the answer was yes.

Apply the same test at work. If it meets those basic criteria, your default should be yes—even when you're not entirely confident you can pull it off.

Try This

Next time an opportunity comes your way that makes you uncomfortable, before you instinctively decline, pause and consider:

  1. Is it legal, moral, and ethical?
  2. What's the worst-case outcome if I try?
  3. What's the best-case outcome if I succeed?
  4. Will I regret not trying?

You might be surprised how often the answer is yes.
