It's OK to Say Sorry (Even When It's Not Your Fault)

The surprising power of apology in professional relationships

I used to think apologizing was admitting defeat.

If I said sorry, I was accepting blame. I was letting the other person win. I was showing weakness.

I was wrong.

Here's what 30 years in tech taught me about the strategic and relational power of saying sorry—even when you're not entirely at fault.

The Myth of the Perfect Defense

Early in my career, when something went wrong, I had one instinct: defend myself.

If a bug shipped, I'd explain why it wasn't caught. If a project ran late, I'd detail all the blockers that weren't my fault. If someone was upset with me, I'd argue my position.

I thought this was professionalism. I was wrong.

What I was actually doing was:

  • Making the other person feel unheard
  • Prolonging conflicts that could have ended quickly
  • Building a reputation as someone who couldn't own mistakes

The "perfect defense" doesn't exist. Even if you win the argument, you often lose the relationship.

Two Kinds of Sorry

The word "sorry" can mean different things:

1. Apologizing for Wrongdoing

"I'm sorry I missed the deadline. I underestimated the complexity and should have flagged it earlier."

This is taking responsibility for something you actually did wrong. It's accepting fault.

2. Expressing Empathy

"I'm sorry this has been so frustrating for you."

This isn't accepting blame—it's acknowledging the other person's experience. It's saying: "I see that you're upset, and I care about that."

The second kind of sorry is vastly underused, especially by technical people who are trained to focus on facts over feelings.

Why Empathic Sorry Works

When someone is upset—whether or not it's your fault—they need to feel heard before they can hear you.

Think about the last time you were frustrated with someone. Did you want them to immediately explain why you shouldn't be frustrated? Or did you want them to acknowledge that the situation was frustrating?

When you say "I'm sorry this has been frustrating," you're not admitting fault. You're saying:

  • I acknowledge your experience
  • I'm not dismissing your feelings
  • I want to understand before I respond

This disarms defensiveness. It creates space for actual problem-solving.

Real Examples

Here's how this plays out in practice:

Scenario: A stakeholder is upset about a delayed feature

Defensive response: "The delay wasn't our fault. The requirements changed three times and we were waiting on the API team for two weeks."

Empathic response: "I'm sorry this delay is impacting your launch. I know how much was riding on this timeline. Let me walk you through what happened and what we're doing to get back on track."

Both responses contain the same information. One creates conflict; the other creates collaboration.

Scenario: A colleague feels left out of a decision

Defensive response: "You were cc'd on the email. The meeting was on the calendar."

Empathic response: "I'm sorry you felt blindsided. That wasn't my intention. Let me catch you up, and I'll make sure to loop you in more directly next time."

Even if you did everything "right," the other person's experience was that something went wrong. Acknowledging that experience costs you nothing and gains you trust.

When to Apologize for Real

Of course, sometimes you actually did something wrong. In those cases:

  1. Be specific: "I'm sorry I didn't communicate the timeline change" is better than "I'm sorry if anyone was confused."
  2. Don't add "but": "I'm sorry, but I was under a lot of pressure" isn't an apology—it's an excuse with an apology wrapper.
  3. Say what you'll do differently: "Going forward, I'll flag timeline risks in our weekly sync."

A genuine apology includes three parts:

  • Acknowledgment of what happened
  • Recognition of impact
  • Commitment to change

The Fear of Looking Weak

I think the reason many people resist apologizing is fear: fear of looking weak, fear of admitting imperfection, fear of giving others ammunition.

In my experience, the opposite is true.

The people I respect most are the ones who can say "I got that wrong" without it threatening their identity. They're secure enough to own mistakes.

The people who never apologize? They come across as fragile—so insecure that they can't tolerate any admission of imperfection.

Owning your mistakes, when you actually make them, is a sign of strength.

The Bottom Line

You don't have to be wrong to say sorry. Sometimes sorry just means: "I care that you're upset, and I want to understand."

And when you are wrong? Say it clearly, without hedging, and commit to doing better.

It's one of the simplest ways to build trust and defuse conflict. And it costs you nothing but a few words.
